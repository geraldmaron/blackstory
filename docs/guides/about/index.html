The human case for a place-connected archive of Black history.

Why BlackStory

Black history is everywhere and somehow still easy to lose. A building gets renamed. A newspaper digitizes and reshuffles its URLs. A local story lives in a basement folder, a family scrapbook, or a grant-funded portal that stops getting updates. Search can find a title. It rarely leaves you with a durable record you can trust on a map.

BlackStory exists for that gap.

The feeling we are building toward

Open the map. Find a place. See a person, a school, a movement, an event pinned with honest precision. Read a short claim. Follow the citation. Notice the confidence grade. Notice when the pin is a block or a locality, not a pretend street address. Leave knowing more than a headline, and knowing how we know it.

The product line is simple on purpose:

History, pinned to place.

People. Places. Evidence. Context.

That is not marketing garnish. It is the design brief. Place comes first. Evidence comes before assertion. Context sits beside records without pretending to be proof of cause. Pride without spectacle. Specific over sweeping. Invite, do not lecture.

What most portals already do well

Discovery portals are excellent at breadth. They help you search across many institutions and jump out to the host catalog. Specialized corpora such as Enslaved.org publish deep, domain-focused linked data we do not try to replace.

Those projects matter. BlackStory is not a rebuke of them.

The empty quadrant we are aiming at

We want entity records: a person, place, or event you can hold in the product. We want those records place-indexed, with map precision that tells the truth. We want general Black history across eras and topics, curated and growing, not a single corpus. And over time we want records that are machine-queryable with stable fields, once quality gates say we are ready.

Most stacks occupy one or two of those axes. The overlap is thinner than it should be. Aggregators widen access and also inherit link rot. When a holding institution migrates its repository, outbound links can break while search results still look fine. Field review of Umbra Search in The American Archivist (April 2026) documents that failure mode clearly.

Our answer is not "aggregators are bad." It is evidence pinned to place, with archived capture pointers so a reader can still open the citation when the origin site moves.

What we refuse

  • Completeness theater. We do not claim to index all Black history. Absence on the map is not proof nothing happened. It may mean sources have not cleared the publish gate yet.
  • Sensational framing. Violence-adjacent records do not become crime-heat maps or alarm-red spectacles.
  • Fake precision. A coarsened point is never labeled as an exact address.
  • Silent living-person risk. Unknown living status is treated as living. Public residential addresses stay out.
  • Automated causation. Showing a law beside a demographic indicator is juxtaposition for context, not a machine claim that the law caused the number. Causal statements need separately evidenced claims.
  • LLM publish paths. Models can help draft and enrich behind the gate. They cannot promote a public release.

How trust is supposed to work

A public page is not a research notebook. Readers see released projections: immutable, reviewed slices of what we are willing to stand behind. Research, quarantine, enrichment, and promotion stay private. Corrections land in intake, not on the live canon.

Confidence stays visible. Color is never the only signal. Citations should carry archived captures before they meet our public evidence bar. That bar measures readiness of the evidence ledger, not coverage of the entire field.

Where we are today

The public product is live and growing. The catalog is on the order of thousands of place-indexed entities, not a finished national inventory. Capture coverage, developer query surfaces, and some cloud wiring are still catching up to the architecture. This docs site is the orientation layer. The repository is the source of truth.

If you are here to contribute, start with Install and bootstrap and the Architecture guide. If you are here to understand the research posture, read Research methodology next.

BlackStory is being built in the open, carefully, with the people and places the history belongs to kept in view.
